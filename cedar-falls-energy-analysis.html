Executive Summary

This analysis compares the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) for natural gas combined cycle power plants against renewable energy sources (utility-scale solar PV and onshore wind) paired with battery storage. LCOE represents the average cost per unit of electricity generated over a plant's lifetime, enabling apples-to-apples comparison across technologies.

Key Finding: Unsubsidized utility-scale solar and onshore wind have been the cheapest forms of new-build electricity generation for 10 consecutive years (Lazard LCOE+ 2025). Even when battery storage is added to firm up intermittent renewables, the combined cost is now competitive with — and increasingly cheaper than — new natural gas combined cycle plants.

What This Means for Cedar Falls

Cedar Falls Utilities (CFU) currently serves approximately 20,871 customer accounts with total retail sales of ~594,000 MWh per year. The utility's generation mix is heavily dependent on coal (~98%) with minimal natural gas. Transitioning toward renewables with battery storage would not only reduce emissions but, based on current cost trajectories, would likely reduce long-term electricity costs for Cedar Falls ratepayers.

Battery storage technology has reached a point where a 4-hour grid-scale system capable of meeting Cedar Falls' peak demand (~120-145 MW) is technically and economically feasible today, with costs projected to drop an additional 40-50% by 2035.

The Crossover Point

The crossover — when new-build renewables became cheaper than new-build natural gas — occurred around 2016-2017 for onshore wind and 2018-2019 for utility-scale solar. Since then, the cost advantage of renewables has generally held, even accounting for the pandemic-era supply chain cost increases of 2022-2024.

Important context: While solar and wind LCOE increased in 2022-2024 due to supply chain disruptions, inflation, and rising interest rates, they remained below or competitive with natural gas combined cycle costs. Natural gas LCOE also rose during this period due to fuel price volatility.

Why Natural Gas Costs Are Rising

Several factors are driving natural gas combined cycle costs upward:

Fuel Price Volatility: Natural gas prices are subject to global market dynamics, geopolitical tensions, and weather events. The 2022 energy crisis demonstrated how quickly prices can spike, directly impacting electricity costs.
Construction Costs: According to Lazard's 2025 report, the cost of building a new combined cycle gas turbine has reached a 10-year high, driven by supply chain constraints and rising material costs.
Anticipated Carbon Costs: As climate policy tightens, explicit or implicit carbon pricing will add to the operating cost of fossil generation. Many utility planning models now incorporate a social cost of carbon ranging from $20–$65/ton CO₂.

Battery Storage: A Revolution in Progress

Battery energy storage systems (BESS) are experiencing one of the most dramatic cost declines in the history of energy technology. Lithium-ion battery pack prices have fallen 93% since 2010, from $1,474/kWh to $108/kWh in 2025 (BloombergNEF).

Technology Trends

The battery storage market is evolving rapidly. Lithium iron phosphate (LFP) chemistry has become dominant for grid-scale applications due to its lower cost ($81/kWh average in 2025), longer cycle life, and improved safety profile compared to nickel manganese cobalt (NMC) chemistries. Long-duration energy storage technologies — including iron-air batteries, compressed air, and flow batteries — are emerging to address multi-day storage needs, with deployments rising 49% in 2025.

Timeline: When Can Battery Storage Supply a City of 50,000?

The answer depends on what "supply" means — and the news is encouraging:

Now — 2026

Feasible today: 4-hour grid-scale battery storage for peak shaving and grid reliability. Systems like the 580 MWh configuration above are well within current technology and commercially proven. Cost: ~$131 million. Iowa's strong wind resources (Iowa generates ~63% of its electricity from wind) make this an excellent pairing.

2027–2030

Highly competitive: Solar+wind+battery hybrid systems become clearly cheaper than any new fossil generation. A combined 200 MW wind + 100 MW solar + 580 MWh battery system could provide ~80% of Cedar Falls' electricity needs. Battery costs drop to ~$165/kWh installed, making the storage component ~$96M.

2030–2035

Near-complete coverage: With 8-hour batteries plus long-duration storage technologies (iron-air, compressed air), 90-95% renewable coverage becomes economically optimal. Total system costs drop significantly as battery installed costs reach ~$142/kWh. Regional grid interconnection handles remaining gaps.

2035–2040

100% renewable feasible: Long-duration storage matures (costs projected to drop 50%+ from current levels), seasonal storage solutions become available, and smart grid management makes full renewable supply for a city of 50,000 technically and economically achievable. The U.S. DOE targets 225–460 GW of long-duration storage nationally by 2050.

Bottom Line for Cedar Falls: A 4-hour battery system to complement renewable generation is feasible and cost-effective right now. Given Iowa's exceptional wind resources, Cedar Falls could begin transitioning immediately with a phased approach — starting with wind + battery, adding solar, and scaling storage as costs continue to decline. By 2030-2035, battery storage paired with renewables will be substantially cheaper than maintaining fossil fuel generation, especially considering CFU's aging coal fleet.

Methodology Notes

LCOE Definition: The Levelized Cost of Energy represents the per-megawatt-hour cost of building and operating a generating plant over an assumed financial life and duty cycle, including capital costs, fuel costs, fixed and variable O&M, financing costs, and an assumed utilization rate. It does not include subsidies unless noted.

Historical Data: Lazard midpoint values are calculated as the average of the low and high end of each technology's unsubsidized LCOE range. IRENA values represent global weighted averages from actual project data.

Projections: Forward projections use NREL ATB 2024 moderate scenario as the primary basis, cross-referenced with EIA AEO 2025 reference case and BNEF forecasts. Natural gas projections incorporate EIA's reference case fuel price assumptions and an anticipated escalation in carbon-related costs consistent with current policy trajectories.

Cedar Falls Demand: Peak demand is estimated using Cedar Falls Utilities' reported 594 GWh annual retail sales, scaled proportionally to a 50,000-person city, with a peak-to-average demand ratio of 1.6–1.8 typical for Midwestern municipal utilities.

Battery Storage Costs: Installed system costs include battery pack, inverter, balance of system, EPC, and developer margin. They are higher than pack-only costs reported by BNEF. NREL moderate scenario projections are used for system-level estimates.
